What Are Calories?
Calories are units of energy provided by food and drink, essential for all living beings.

Humans need energy to survive and function daily. To manage weight, whether gaining or losing, adjusting caloric intake is key.

Factors such as assigned gender at birth, age, and physical activity level can influence the number of calories needed. Generally, a healthy daily intake ranges from 1,600 to 3,000 kilocalories. On food packaging, the term 'calories' often actually refers to kilocalories.

For personalized guidance on safely adjusting your eating habits, consult a dietary professional. Additional information can be found on HeathClevelandClinic.org.



Cow-lorie Clock
Similar to humans, the agriculture industry relies on calories to raise livestock. In this blog, we'll compare the caloric intake of cows to that of humans.

Cattle, which make up a significant portion of the meat and dairy industries, number over 1.55 billion worldwide (as of 2022, Statista.com).

Maintaining such a large population requires substantial calories and resources. On average, a cow requires about 18,000 kilocalories per day, while dairy cows need around 23,000 kilocalories (CalesArizona.edu).

Cow to Produce Conversions
As illustrated above, cows are quite calorie-intensive, requiring approximately 6 to 10 times more calories than humans. Although the global population of cows is smaller than that of humans, their total calorie consumption still surpasses the average calorie intake of all humans combined.

So, what do we gain from this high caloric investment? Cows provide us with milk and meat. On average, a dairy cow produces between 6 to 8 gallons of milk daily (CowCareTaker.com), for the 10 out of 12 months which they lactate (InformedFarmers.com), with each gallon containing about 2,375 kilocalories (YourCalories.net).

Additionally, at the end of a cow's life (1.5 years for non-dairy, 18 years for dairy), approximately 430 pounds of meat is harvested (SimpsonsMeats.com), with each pound containing about 200 kilocalories (CheatDayDesign.com).

According to the laws of energy, we can never obtain more energy than what is invested, so the total energy derived from cows will always be less than the calories they consume.

In ideal conditions, approximately 30% of the calories invested in raising a dairy cow are lost. On average, this translates to discarding about 6,900 Kilo-Calories from the 23,000 Kilo-Calories invested in a dairy cow each day. This amount of lost energy is roughly three times the average daily caloric intake of a person.

The energy loss becomes even more pronounced with cattle raised solely for meat, where over 98% of the invested calories are lost. This amount of lost energy per day is roughly six times the average daily caloric intake of a person.



Closing Thoughts
While livestock agriculture faces various criticisms, this blog has focused specifically on the caloric investment and returns from cattle.

Each year, approximately 9 million people die from hunger and hunger-related diseases (TheWorldCounts.com), while cattle are supplied with large amounts of calories, only to have their lives cut short before reaching two years of age.

Although dairy cows are more efficient in their caloric use compared to those raised exclusively for meat, the calories lost from just the dairy cows over the span of 2 days** in the U.S. could theoretically provide the average daily requirement of 2,500 Kilo Calories to all 9 million people in need.

This stark contrast highlights the inefficiencies in our food systems and underscores the need for more sustainable practices.
